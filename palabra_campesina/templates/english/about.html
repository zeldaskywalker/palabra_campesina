ABOUT PALABRA

Palabra Campesina is a community-based project that honors the lives, voices, and stories of farmworker communities in Watsonville. Rooted in the oral tradition, this project gathers testimonies from people who live or have lived in labor camps, migrant housing, or agricultural neighborhoods in the Pájaro Valley.

These communities, shaped over generations of migration and labor in California's agricultural fields, have often been ignored by the media and official records. Palabra Campesina seeks to counter this invisibility by creating a living archive of testimonies—stories of migration, struggle, joy, family, resilience, and everyday life.

Through live storytelling events, oral history interviews, photography, and community gatherings, Palabra Campesina creates spaces where farmworker voices can be heard and honored on their own terms.

The project was born from the belief that telling our stories not only preserves culture, but also heals, makes us visible, and connects generations. It also highlights the continued presence of agricultural labor camps in our region—many of which remain active today and function as permanent housing, despite having been designed as seasonal shelters.

We invite you to get to know the heart of Watsonville's agricultural communities and to listen to the stories of the people whose labor feeds us all.


THE CREATOR

Alex Rocha-Álvarez is the daughter of Jardines del Valle, previously known as Murphy Camp. As a child, she knew it as "the camp in the hole," because from the street, her home looked as if it were sunken into the ground. Created around 1920 as a farm labor camp, her home has always belonged to farmworkers.

In the Pájaro Valley, there are many camps like hers, hidden among strawberry fields and rural roads. If you don't look closely, these camps are almost invisible—and so are the stories of the people who live there.

But Alex believes we all have something to learn from farmworker communities. She knows this intimately because this community raised her. It was in "the camp in the hole" that she learned there is no heart like a farmworker’s. Palabra Campesina was born from her belief that—if we have the wisdom to listen—we all have something to learn from farmworkers.

Alex is a writer, scholar, and community arts organizer. As a first-generation college student, she graduated with honors from Yale University, where she deepened her work on labor, migration, and resilience through both coursework and independent projects.

She is currently a PhD student at the University of California, Berkeley, where she continues her commitment to the farmworker communities of the Pájaro Valley. Her research explores the impact of agricultural and housing policies from the official dismantling of labor camps in the 1970s to the present. Through her academic work, she seeks to recover hidden histories of adaptation and transformation and to use that knowledge in service of her community.


COLLABORATORS

Lead Photographer: Ángela Higuera

Both of Ángela's parents come from farmworker families in Mexico, but after immigrating to the United States, those agricultural roots were no longer a common topic in family conversations. For Ángela, reconnecting with this heritage has been an essential part of her personal and creative journey. As the lead photographer for Palabra Campesina, she uses photography as a tool to explore the relationship between land, food, and justice for agricultural communities. Ángela graduated from Yale University in 2023 with a BA in American Studies. She currently works in after school education, supporting young people in their growth and development.


Web Developer: Zelda Montes

Zelda Montes is a labor organizer and digital engineer committed to creating digital projects in service of community. They are currently pursuing a master’s degree in Digital Humanities at CUNY, where they study how digital archives can become spaces of resistance and collective memory. They have worked on projects such as More than Surviving, which recovers the political agency of the Wampanoag Tribe, and the NOTA Google NYC Digital Memory Archive, which documents labor organizing in response to the tech industry's complicity in the genocide of the Palestinian people.


Graphic Designer: Eda Uzunlar

Eda Uzunlar is a journalist and graphic designer from South Dakota and a first-generation American with Turkish roots. She creates art and design to tell accessible stories about culture and community. Learn more about her work at euzunlar.com.
